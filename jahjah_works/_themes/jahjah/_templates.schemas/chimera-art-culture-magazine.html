Houston, TX

Jah Jah for Chimera Art & Culture Magazine

by M!ecstatic
August 5, 2014

Jah Jah is a twenty-four year old Houston-based artist whose paintings are mind-bending in nature. His lines create cerebral mazes that might be described as tribal, futuristic and sometimes incredibly psychedelic. Some of his work is almost chaotic at first glance, but if you look a little closer you’ll find geometrical patterns and symbols which he has conjured over the last few years. His work can be found in the crevices of Houston, including a recent mural at The Silo near Last Concert Café in the Warehouse district. Currently, Jah Jah is working with the Uriel Landeros to create a mission statement for their collective art group, Annunaki. He will also be participating in a collaborative show with local artists Juan Carlos and Nina Marinick in November.

M!ecstatic M

What’s your name?

Jah Jah J

My name is originally Joshua Kenneth Gray, but most people refer to me as Jah Jah.

M!ecstatic M

So where are we?

Jah Jah J

This is where I do most of my painting. It’s an old garage that I turned into a studio space. I’ve been painting here for the past two years- on and off, here and there.

M!ecstatic M

What is your preferred medium?

Jah Jah J

Right now painting is my focus but I spend a lot of my time drawing, using pencil and pen on paper. I don’t do a lot of photography or even sculpture, or anything three-dimensional. It’s mostly drawing and painting at the moment.

M!ecstatic M

Can you remember the moment when you became aware of lines, colors and the ability to manipulate them into something you wanted to see?

Jah Jah J

Growing up I was always encouraged to keep on painting and keep drawing and I guess I can say throughout all my training in school. Being on my own with my art outside of school, I don’t think it came until a couple of years ago when I just kind of decided to throw away everything I knew, or thought I knew, about art and created my own way of doing it. So I neglected everything I learned and freed my hand up. A lot of the art I was making looked kind of childlike and then the more I did it, the more organized it became and it sort of evolved into this thing that you see now, something that’s based off of intuition, free-form and spontaneity.

M!ecstatic M

Do you use a certain method each time you begin a project? Is it just a free-flow? Or both?

Jah Jah J

The development begins inherently as a free-flowing thing and the more [time] I spend not being concerned with where I’m going the more things are shown to me. And these processes that come out of not having any, or having little control over what’s next, I create processes out of that. So each new thing that I do, or each new process that I try or come across is one extra thing I have in my database of processes and things that I can do. They kind of form each other- the free-flowing and the more compositional aspect.

M!ecstatic M

Can you describe the feeling you get when you are in that flow? Would you call it the “creative force” taking over, or are you always aware of your next move?

Jah Jah J

There is a feeling and it’s that feeling that I’ve been chasing and that two years ago I finally found, that I was able to channel at will whenever I picked up any type of writing or creating utensil. It’s a feeling that this could go on forever, unstopped without any type of boost or help from any outside… anything- and it could sustain itself without dying out, that feeling of infinity or forever existing. When I’m in that zone it’s like each brush stroke could be the equivalent to an inhale or an exhale. You can actually create on a piece of paper or canvas the same you can create a dance piece or a whole theater performance.

M!ecstatic M

How do you feel reality and spirituality play a role in your work?

Jah Jah J

Personally, I feel like when it comes to energy and these concepts such as spirit, I think when you’re creating, drawing or painting you’re engaging in a form of meditation. And meditation in most religions has been proclaimed to be the one thing that you can do to tap into a universal energy field, or [to] see everything as one. I have a feeling that maybe when you create, you connect yourself to that. One the biggest aspects of creating is just being present at the moment that you’re doing it.

M!ecstatic M

What people, works or aesthetics inspire you?

Jah Jah J

When I look at people who inspire me, it’s not necessarily the work of these people that inspires me, it’s really the work ethic, to see how these people went about their lives or how they went about creating whatever it is that they create. Looking at what was produced based of how much energy and time was put into it, it’s really that that I’m inspired by when it comes to other people. When it comes to techniques, I don’t have very many people who have influenced me. I have looked at Escher before and I’ve seen some similarities in how geometry is used and as far as subject matter I’m not really influenced by many other artists when it comes down to [it]. I tend to pull from ideas that I come up with and things that are inside me. There are particular symbols that I’ve used though. And I guess I have this sort of database of symbols that I’ve created and that I’ve borrowed.

M!ecstatic M

What are some examples of symbols that you use?

Jah Jah J

One symbol is the symbol of a bull. That’s something that another artist Uriel Landeros uses, and it’s also a symbol that Picasso used. I also have imagery of birds. A symbol I use frequently is a symbol for a woman, it is an upside down triangle. Some of the symbols are personal, especially my logo. There are some symbols that are more conventional- an infinity sign, a cross, a star- there’s one for water, a symbol for wind, a circle inside a circle is a common one. When I’m working in my journal and have thoughts, I record them- sometimes I think about symbols or I have to write down a slogan or just some sentence that I think of and in a way I use that as a reminder, so these words that I come up with become assigned a word or phrase and ultimately I write down that symbol and I know what is associated with it so quietly informs me every time I see it.

M!ecstatic M

Where would you like to show your work?

Jah Jah J

So I was in Ghana for two months and there’s Christian religious propaganda everywhere and corporate propaganda everywhere, things on billboards that are just very Christian or very corporate. So one idea that I really want to do….I don’t know when or how far in the future I’ll be able to do this; I want to do a campaign where I’m wheat pasting and putting up my own billboards where I’m combatting those Ideas presented and the religious propaganda and corporate billboards.

M!ecstatic M

Have you collaborated with other local artists?

Jah Jah J

Recently I’ve been collaborating with a few people- Nina Marinick and Juan Carlos. And before that Uriel Landeros, who is in jail right now. He’s serving for spray-painting a Picasso piece. He’ll be out in November of this year. I don’t know the details of our collaboration, but I will definitely be collaborating with him when he gets out. We started a group called the Annunaki. Right now we are writing our mission statement. That statement will have explanations about the future objectives of our group.

We have a mission to combat, as much as we can with what’s in our creative power, the brainwashing that’s going on here in America. We have a mission to do what’s best in our ability to raise awareness of the situation not only in the United States but the whole world. A lot of these things we want to promote are things that a lot of people are aware of and a lot who aren’t and those who are aware don’t feel it’s in their power to help - or maybe they just don’t care to help the situation of the world.

M!ecstatic M

Where can we see your work? Do you have any events coming up?

Jah Jah J

I just did a mural at The Silo, near last concert. In Novermber I’ll be doing a collaborative show with Juan Carlos and Nina Marinick.
